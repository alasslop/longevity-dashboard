I scored below 25% on metabolic markers — what does this mean?

3 min read
Poor metabolic health is alarmingly common — and it doubles your cardiovascular disease risk while increasing all-cause mortality by 58%...
Quick Answer
Metabolic dysfunction is now the norm, not the exception: only 12.2% of American adults are metabolically healthy across all five criteria (Araújo et al., NHANES). The metabolic syndrome — having 3 or more abnormal markers — doubles CVD risk (RR 2.35) and increases all-cause mortality by 58%. The critical insight: metabolic health is highly responsive to lifestyle intervention.

The Araújo et al. NHANES analysis revealed that fewer than 1 in 8 Americans meet optimal thresholds for blood glucose, triglycerides, HDL, blood pressure, and waist circumference. This prevalence declined from 19.9% to 12.2% between 2009 and 2016, reflecting worsening population metabolic health.

Mottillo et al.'s meta-analysis of 87 studies and 951,083 participants confirmed the clinical significance: metabolic syndrome doubles cardiovascular disease risk and raises all-cause mortality by 58%. Importantly, individual components — particularly elevated triglycerides and low HDL — each independently predict cardiovascular events even below the metabolic syndrome threshold.

Casey Means frames metabolic health as foundational: 'Nearly every health problem we face can be explained by how well the cells in our body create and use energy.' Poor metabolic markers signal that cellular energy processing is impaired — affecting not just cardiovascular health but also cognitive function, immune response, and aging.

Why is fasting insulin the earliest metabolic warning sign?

4 min read
Insulin resistance develops years — sometimes decades — before fasting glucose rises. By the time glucose is abnormal, the metabolic damage is already advanced...
Quick Answer
Fasting insulin rises years before fasting glucose becomes abnormal. In the Malmö Preventive Project, men in the highest insulin quartile had a 58% higher all-cause mortality risk — even after adjusting for glucose, BMI, and traditional risk factors. Peter Attia considers insulin the most sensitive early marker of metabolic dysfunction, calling it 'the canary in the coal mine.'

The biological sequence is critical to understand: as cells become insulin resistant, the pancreas compensates by producing more insulin. For years, this hyperinsulinemia keeps blood glucose in the normal range — but at a metabolic cost. Elevated insulin drives fat storage, raises triglycerides, suppresses HDL, and promotes inflammation — all while glucose tests look 'normal.'

Hedblad et al. followed 6,074 men for 19 years in the Malmö Preventive Project and found that fasting hyperinsulinemia independently predicted both all-cause and cardiovascular mortality, even after controlling for glucose and traditional risk factors. This demonstrates that insulin itself — not just the glucose it regulates — carries prognostic information.

Peter Attia targets fasting insulin of 2-6 µIU/mL (optimal) and considers values above 7-8 µIU/mL as early evidence of insulin resistance requiring intervention. He emphasizes that standard medical practice often ignores insulin until glucose becomes abnormal — by which point metabolic dysfunction has been progressing for 5-15 years.

This is why fasting insulin receives the highest weight (25%) in the metabolic sub-score: it's the earliest detectable signal and the most actionable point for intervention.

What do my fasting glucose numbers really mean?

3 min read
The conventional 'normal' cutoff of 100 mg/dL may already be too high — risk begins to rise well below the prediabetes threshold...
Quick Answer
Mortality risk rises continuously from normal glucose levels upward — there's no safe 'threshold.' The DECODE study found that even impaired fasting glucose (110-125 mg/dL) carries 21% higher mortality risk. Casey Means targets an optimal range of 70-85 mg/dL — significantly below the conventional 'normal' cutoff of 100 mg/dL.

The DECODE collaboration analyzed 29,714 participants across 13 European cohorts and demonstrated a graded, continuous relationship between fasting glucose and mortality. Crucially, risk begins to increase at glucose levels well below the diabetes diagnostic threshold of 126 mg/dL — even the 'normal' range shows gradation.

This finding aligns with Peter Attia's emphasis on the oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) as a more sensitive measure: fasting glucose may appear normal while post-meal glucose excursions reveal early metabolic dysfunction. By the time fasting glucose exceeds 100 mg/dL, significant insulin resistance has likely been present for years.

The practical implication: don't be reassured by a 'normal' fasting glucose of 95 mg/dL. Combined with your fasting insulin level, glucose provides a picture of how hard your body is working to maintain blood sugar homeostasis.

How do triglycerides reflect metabolic health?

3 min read
Elevated triglycerides are a direct signal that insulin is failing to regulate fat metabolism — making them a bridge between metabolic and cardiovascular risk...
Quick Answer
Gerald Reaven's landmark research identified the triglyceride-HDL connection as a hallmark of insulin resistance. When insulin fails to suppress fat release from adipose tissue, triglycerides rise and HDL falls — creating the atherogenic dyslipidemia pattern. The metabolic syndrome (which doubles CVD risk) uses triglycerides ≥150 mg/dL as one of its five diagnostic criteria. Casey Means targets <80 mg/dL as optimal.

Reaven's 1988 Banting Lecture defined 'Syndrome X' — now called metabolic syndrome — as a cluster of metabolic abnormalities driven by insulin resistance. The core triad: elevated triglycerides, low HDL, and hyperglycemia. This paper fundamentally changed our understanding of how these markers connect.

Triglycerides respond rapidly to dietary changes, making them an excellent feedback marker. Reducing refined carbohydrates — particularly sugar, white flour, and sweetened beverages — can lower triglycerides by 20-40% within 2-4 weeks. This makes triglycerides one of the fastest-responding metabolic markers.

In the LongevityPath scoring, triglycerides contribute to both your cardiovascular score (15% weight, reflecting atherogenic dyslipidemia) and your metabolic score (25% weight, reflecting insulin resistance). This dual contribution mirrors their biological role as a bridge between metabolic and cardiovascular health.

Why is HDL important for metabolic health?

3 min read
Low HDL is one of the five metabolic syndrome criteria — and it tracks closely with insulin resistance and elevated triglycerides...
Quick Answer
Low HDL cholesterol is both a metabolic syndrome criterion and an independent cardiovascular risk factor. In insulin-resistant states, HDL production decreases while HDL clearance increases — creating a characteristic pattern of low HDL paired with high triglycerides. This TG/HDL ratio is one of the most powerful simple predictors of metabolic and cardiovascular risk.

HDL and triglycerides move in opposite directions because they share the same metabolic pathway: cholesterol ester transfer protein (CETP) exchanges triglycerides from VLDL for cholesterol esters from HDL. When VLDL (triglyceride-rich) particles are abundant — as in insulin resistance — this exchange is accelerated, depleting HDL of cholesterol and loading it with triglycerides that mark it for rapid clearance.

This is why the TG/HDL ratio is so informative: it captures both sides of the metabolic equation in a single number. A ratio below 2:1 suggests healthy insulin sensitivity; above 3:1 signals significant metabolic dysfunction. Casey Means highlights this ratio as particularly predictive of cardiovascular events.

In the metabolic sub-score, HDL carries 25% weight. Combined with its 15% weight in the cardiovascular sub-score, HDL reflects its dual role as both a metabolic indicator and a cardiovascular protector.

Casey Means' framework uses five simple biomarkers to define metabolic health — and her research shows fewer than 1 in 8 Americans pass all five...
Quick Answer
Casey Means (Good Energy) defines metabolic health using five markers with optimal thresholds: fasting glucose 70-85 mg/dL, HbA1c <5.4%, triglycerides <80 mg/dL, HDL 50-90 mg/dL, waist circumference <88cm (women) / <102cm (men). An NHANES analysis found only 12.2% of American adults meet all five criteria — declining from 19.9% just seven years earlier.

How can I improve my metabolic markers?

4 min read
The Diabetes Prevention Program proved that lifestyle beats medication for metabolic health — and the improvements come faster than most people expect...
Quick Answer
Lifestyle intervention is the most powerful tool for metabolic improvement. The Diabetes Prevention Program showed that diet and exercise reduced diabetes incidence by 58% — outperforming metformin (31%). Fasting insulin and triglycerides can improve within 2-4 weeks of dietary changes. The key targets: reduce refined carbohydrates, increase physical activity, optimize sleep, and manage stress.

The Diabetes Prevention Program (Knowler et al., 2002) remains one of the most important lifestyle intervention trials. In 3,234 adults with impaired glucose tolerance, moderate lifestyle changes — 150 minutes of weekly exercise and 7% weight loss — reduced diabetes incidence by 58% over 2.8 years. This outperformed metformin (31% reduction), demonstrating that metabolic health responds more strongly to behavior than to medication.

For fasting insulin specifically, the fastest improvements come from reducing refined carbohydrate intake and increasing physical activity. Both resistance training and aerobic exercise independently improve insulin sensitivity — and the combination is additive. Even a single exercise session can measurably improve insulin sensitivity for 24-48 hours.

Sleep is an underappreciated metabolic lever. Even one night of sleep deprivation can reduce insulin sensitivity by 25-30%. Optimizing sleep duration (7-8 hours), consistency, and quality has direct metabolic benefits that compound with dietary and exercise improvements.
