I scored below 25% on mindset — what does that mean for my health?

3 min read · Based on 2 studies
A low mindset score signals barriers to lasting health behavior change — and sustained healthy behaviors reduce all-cause mortality by up to 66%...
Quick Answer
A low mindset score doesn't predict your health directly — it predicts whether other health improvements will stick. People who sustain at least four healthy behaviors reduce all-cause mortality by 66%, but sustaining them requires the readiness, grit, and growth orientation this section measures.
Meta-Analysis published in Preventive Medicine
The Combined Effects of Healthy Lifestyle Behaviors on All Cause Mortality: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
Loef & Walach, 2012 · 15 studies, n=531,804 · DOI: 10.1016/j.ypmed.2012.06.017
Key finding: A combination of at least four healthy lifestyle factors was associated with a 66% reduction in all-cause mortality risk. The relative risks decreased proportionately with each additional healthy behavior adopted.

The gap between knowing what's healthy and actually doing it is largely a mindset gap. Stage of readiness strongly predicts whether health interventions succeed — people in early stages of change make significantly less progress regardless of the quality of the program [1].

The Good News
Every component of your mindset score is modifiable. Change readiness builds through small wins. Grit develops through deliberate practice. Growth mindset responds to brief interventions. A low score is a starting point — it tells you exactly where to focus before investing in diet plans or gym memberships that won't stick without the psychological foundation.

Why do we ask about change readiness — and what does each checkbox mean?

4 min read · Based on 2 studies
The three checkboxes map to three components from behavior change theory — awareness, responsibility, and self-efficacy. Each missing component has a different solution...
Quick Answer
Change readiness is one of the strongest predictors of whether health interventions succeed. Stage-matched interventions produce clinically significant effects (d = 0.46). The three checkboxes diagnose exactly which component is missing — so we can coach the right thing.

The Transtheoretical Model (Prochaska & Velicer, 1997) shows that behavior change is not a single event but a process through stages — and that matching the intervention to the person's stage dramatically improves outcomes. Our three checkboxes map directly to what separates the stages:

"Something has to change" = Awareness. Without this, you're in the precontemplation stage — not yet seeing the need. People here benefit from information about risks and consequences, not action plans. Exploring the evidence pages in this assessment can shift this component.

"I'm the one who must change it" = Personal responsibility. You see the problem but attribute control to genetics, circumstances, or healthcare providers. Shifting from external to internal attribution — recognizing that your daily choices shape your trajectory — is the bridge from contemplation to preparation.

"I believe I can do it" = Self-efficacy. This is the most researched component. Bandura's foundational work established that self-efficacy is the strongest single predictor of whether someone initiates and maintains health behavior change. Low self-efficacy responds best to mastery experiences — small, achievable wins that build confidence incrementally.

Meta-Analysis published in Journal of Clinical Psychology
Stages of Change and Psychotherapy Outcomes: A Review and Meta-Analysis
Krebs, Norcross, Nicholson & Prochaska, 2018 · 39 studies, n=8,238 · DOI: 10.1002/jclp.22683
Key finding: Stage of change predicted therapy outcomes with a clinically significant effect size (d = 0.46). Progress is a function of pretreatment readiness — not just willpower or access to information.
View Study
What To Do Based on Your Checkboxes
0 boxes: Start with education — explore the evidence pages. Don't pressure yourself into action yet.

1 box (awareness only): You see the problem but don't feel ownership or confidence. Start with one tiny commitment you control entirely — a 10-minute walk — to build both ownership and self-efficacy simultaneously.

2 boxes (missing self-efficacy): Focus on small wins — things so easy they feel trivial. Mastery experiences are the most powerful source of self-efficacy [1].

All 3 boxes: You're ready. Move on to building grit and committing time to prevention.
References
[1] Bandura, A. (1977). "Self-Efficacy: Toward a Unifying Theory of Behavioral Change." Psychological Review, 84(2), 191–215. doi:10.1037/0033-295X.84.2.191

Why does it matter how long I've maintained my health habits?

3 min read · Based on 2 studies
Most people who start exercising quit within the first year. The 6-month and 2-year marks are critical transitions that predict whether habits become permanent...
Quick Answer
Only 25–45% of people who start exercising are still doing it after one year. The first 6 months are the initiation phase with the highest dropout risk; reaching 2+ years signals that the behavior has become part of your identity. Duration of maintenance is one of the strongest predictors of whether a health habit will last.

Behavior change theory identifies 6 months as the benchmark for transitioning from "action" to "maintenance." Before this point, exercise relapse curves look similar to addiction relapse curves — the majority of dropouts happen in the first several months, with rates eventually leveling off between 55% and 75% attrition within the first year [1].

Reaching the 2-year mark is significant because it signals a shift from effortful behavior to automatic habit. Research on habit formation shows that instigation habits — the automatic triggering of exercise rather than deliberating each time — are what sustain long-term adherence. Longer maintenance strengthens these automatic patterns [2].

Review published in Health Psychology Review
Theoretical Explanations for Maintenance of Behaviour Change: A Systematic Review of Behaviour Theories
Kwasnicka et al., 2016 · DOI: 10.1080/17437199.2016.1151372
Key finding: Five broad themes explain long-term behavior maintenance: maintenance motives, self-regulation, habit, resources, and environmental/social influences. Habit formation was identified as a critical mechanism — once a behavior becomes automatic, it requires less cognitive effort and is more resistant to disruption.
View Study

This is why the scoring gives 0 points for "haven't started yet," 3 for less than 6 months, 7 for 6 months to 2 years, and full marks for over 2 years. Each threshold reflects a meaningful transition in the psychology of behavior maintenance — not an arbitrary cutoff.

References
[1] Dishman, R.K. (1982). "Compliance/Adherence in Health-Related Exercise." Health Psychology, 1(3), 237–267. doi:10.1037/0278-6133.1.3.237
[2] Gardner, B., et al. (2021). "A Systematic Review Examining the Relationship Between Habit and Physical Activity Behavior in Longitudinal Studies." Frontiers in Psychology, 12, 626750. doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2021.626750

Why does your response to setbacks matter so much?

3 min read · Based on 2 studies
A single missed workout is a lapse — not a failure. Research shows the difference between lapse and relapse is how quickly you recover, not whether the lapse happens...
Quick Answer
Everyone lapses — what separates long-term success from failure is recovery speed. Behavior change research distinguishes between a "lapse" (temporary slip) and a "relapse" (complete return to old behavior). The critical factor is your coping response: getting back on track within a day prevents the spiral from lapse to relapse.

Marlatt's Relapse Prevention model — originally developed for addiction but now widely applied to health behaviors — shows that a lapse is not a dead end but a fork in the road. The person's response to the lapse determines whether it becomes a full relapse or a brief interruption. People with high recovery self-efficacy are far less likely to let a single missed workout spiral into months of inactivity [1].

Systematic Review published in Psychology & Health
Predictors of Lapse and Relapse in Physical Activity and Dietary Behaviour
Hagger et al., 2022 · 37(10), 1289–1309 · DOI: 10.1080/08870446.2021.1981900
Key finding: Psychosocial constructs — particularly self-efficacy and coping planning — were among the strongest predictors of whether a lapse escalated to full relapse. People who planned how to handle setbacks in advance were significantly more likely to recover quickly.
View Study

Exercise relapse curves show that the probability of lapse is 18% after the first month and rises to 34% after four months [2]. Lapses are expected, not exceptional. This is why our assessment asks what you typically do after a disruption — someone who gets back on track the next day has a fundamentally different health trajectory than someone who takes weeks to restart or gives up entirely.

Build a Recovery Plan
The single most effective strategy is coping planning — deciding in advance what you'll do when a lapse happens. For example: "If I miss my workout, I'll do a 10-minute walk instead" or "If I eat poorly at lunch, dinner stays on plan." Having a plan reduces the cognitive load of recovery, making it automatic rather than a decision you need willpower to make.
References
[1] Hendershot, C.S., Witkiewitz, K., George, W.H. & Marlatt, G.A. (2011). "Relapse Prevention for Addictive Behaviors." Substance Abuse Treatment, Prevention, and Policy, 6, 17. doi:10.1186/1747-597X-6-17
[2] Pekmezi, D., et al. (2021). "Lapse, Relapse, and Recovery in Physical Activity Interventions for Latinas: A Survival Analysis." International Journal of Behavioral Medicine, 28(5), 583–593. doi:10.1007/s12529-020-09948-2

Why do your beliefs about health improvement matter?

3 min read · Based on 2 studies
Believing your health is improvable through effort — a growth mindset — predicts whether you actually engage in health behaviors. And this belief is itself changeable...
Quick Answer
Carol Dweck's research distinguishes a growth mindset (abilities can be developed) from a fixed mindset (abilities are innate). Applied to health, growth mindset positively predicts active health behaviors (β = 0.256) and subjective wellbeing (β = 0.306) in older adults. Your beliefs about health shape the effort you put in — and the outcomes you achieve.
Validation Study published in Journal of Happiness Studies
Development and Validation of the Growth Mindset Scale in Older Adults
Xiao et al., 2025 · DOI: 10.1007/s10902-025-00980-7
Key finding: Growth mindset predicted active health behaviors across six domains — interpersonal relations, nutrition, health responsibility, physical activity, stress management, and spiritual growth. The relationship was significant even after controlling for demographics.
View Study

The good news: growth mindset is itself malleable. A meta-analysis of 53 intervention studies found meaningful effects on mental health (d = 0.32) and behavior, with the largest national trial (n=12,490) showing improved academic outcomes from a brief online intervention [1]. The irony of a "fixed" growth mindset would contradict its own premise.

Important Nuance
The growth mindset literature has faced legitimate methodological criticism — a competing meta-analysis found smaller effects and raised concerns about researcher allegiance. The field consensus is that growth mindset interventions work, but effects are modest and context-dependent. This is why we measure mindset as one of several dimensions, not a magic bullet.

The practical takeaway: if you answered "my health is mostly genetic" or "largely out of my control," your beliefs may be limiting the effort you invest — which then confirms the belief. Breaking this cycle doesn't require blind optimism. Start with one area where effort clearly produces results (like strength training or sleep hygiene) and let the evidence of your own improvement shift the belief.

References
[1] Burnette, J.L., et al. (2023). "A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Growth Mindset Interventions." Psychological Bulletin, 149(3-4), 174–205. doi:10.1037/bul0000368

Why do we ask how many hours you invest in preventive health?

3 min read · Based on 2 studies
The dose-response relationship between physical activity and mortality is steep — even 5 extra minutes per day meaningfully reduces mortality risk...
Quick Answer
Time is the currency of prevention. Meeting the recommended 150–300 minutes/week of moderate activity reduces all-cause mortality by 20–21%. Doing 2–4 times that amount provides further reductions. Even a 5-minute/day increase among the least active could prevent 6% of all deaths.
Pooled Analysis published in JAMA Internal Medicine
Leisure Time Physical Activity and Mortality: A Detailed Pooled Analysis of the Dose-Response Relationship
Arem et al., 2015 · 6 cohorts, n=661,137 · DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2015.0533
Key finding: Meeting the minimum recommended guideline (7.5 MET-h/wk, ~150 min moderate activity) was associated with a 20% lower mortality risk. Maximum benefit was observed at 3–5 times the recommended minimum (22.5–40 MET-h/wk), with a 39% risk reduction. No harmful upper limit was identified.
View Study

Our scoring reflects this dose-response curve: "almost none" (0 points) captures the highest-risk group; "under 2 hours" (3 points) is below guidelines; "2–4 hours" (7 points) meets the minimum; and "5+ hours" (10 points) approaches the range of maximum benefit. The question includes all preventive activities — not just exercise — because meal prep, health education, meditation, and recovery time all contribute to your longevity investment.

If your answer was "almost none" or "under 2 hours," the return on even a small increase is enormous. A recent Lancet meta-analysis found that adding just 5 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity per day among the least active participants could prevent 6% of all deaths [1]. You don't need to become an athlete — you need to start.

References
[1] Ekelund, U., Tarp, J., Ding, D., et al. (2026). "Deaths Potentially Averted by Small Changes in Physical Activity and Sedentary Time: An Individual Participant Data Meta-Analysis of Prospective Cohort Studies." The Lancet, 407(10425), 396–406. doi:10.1016/S0140-6736(25)02219-6

Why does a preventive orientation matter more than treating problems?

3 min read · Based on 1 study
Combining even a few preventive health behaviors reduces all-cause mortality by up to 66% — a larger effect than most pharmaceutical interventions...
Quick Answer
Prevention offers returns that reactive treatment cannot match. Combining four healthy lifestyle behaviors reduces all-cause mortality by 66% — a larger risk reduction than most medications achieve. This question measures whether your default orientation is to invest in health proactively or to wait until problems force action.
Meta-Analysis published in Preventive Medicine
The Combined Effects of Healthy Lifestyle Behaviors on All Cause Mortality
Loef & Walach, 2012 · 15 studies, n=531,804 · DOI: 10.1016/j.ypmed.2012.06.017
Key finding: Each additional healthy lifestyle behavior adopted further reduced mortality risk, with four or more combined behaviors yielding a 66% reduction. The dose-response was clear and consistent across studies.
View Study

The difference between a preventive and reactive orientation is often the difference between managing health versus managing disease. By the time symptoms force action — high blood pressure, prediabetes, loss of muscle mass — years of preventable decline have already occurred. Prevention catches the trajectory early, when small interventions produce the largest effects.

If you answered "I mostly react when issues come up" or "I only deal with health when I have to," this doesn't make you unusual — it's the default for most people. But shifting even slightly toward prevention is one of the highest-leverage changes you can make. Start by scheduling one proactive health activity per week and treating it as non-negotiable.

References
[1] Loef, M. & Walach, H. (2012). "The Combined Effects of Healthy Lifestyle Behaviors on All Cause Mortality: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." Preventive Medicine, 55(3), 163–170. doi:10.1016/j.ypmed.2012.06.017

What are the most evidence-backed ways to improve my mindset score?

3 min read · Based on 2 studies
Each subsection responds to different strategies — from mastery experiences for self-efficacy to coping planning for setback recovery...
Quick Answer
Work on the subsections in order: Change Readiness → Growth Mindset → Grit → Prevention. There's no point building grit for health behaviors if you haven't decided change is needed. Build the foundation first.

Change Readiness: Missing boxes? See the coaching guide in FAQ #3. Missing awareness → explore evidence. Missing responsibility → start with one thing you control. Missing self-efficacy → tiny wins.

Grit (Habit Duration): Choose activities you enjoy (not just what's "optimal"). Set process goals ("exercise 4x this week") rather than outcome goals ("lose 5 kg"). Build identity-based habits — "I'm someone who exercises" is more durable than "I'm trying to exercise."

Grit (Setback Recovery): Create a coping plan before the lapse happens. "If I miss my workout, I'll do a 10-minute walk instead." Research shows that pre-planned coping responses dramatically reduce the chance of a lapse becoming a relapse [1].

Growth Mindset: When you fail at a health goal, ask "what can I learn?" rather than "I can't do this." Seek out one area where effort clearly produces results (like strength training) to let experience shift your beliefs.

Preventive Health: Schedule one proactive health activity weekly and protect that time. Use this assessment periodically to track improvement — seeing progress reinforces the value of prevention.

Priority Order
Readiness → Mindset → Grit → Prevention. Building grit for health behaviors before you believe change is possible leads to frustration. Investing in prevention before you have the habit infrastructure leads to wasted resources. Get the psychological foundation right first.
References
[1] Hagger, M.S., et al. (2022). "Predictors of Lapse and Relapse in Physical Activity and Dietary Behaviour." Psychology & Health, 37(10), 1289–1309. doi:10.1080/08870446.2021.1981900
