Protein: Questions & Answers

Evidence-based answers to common protein questions

The Bottom Line

Adults 40+ should aim for 1.2–1.6 g protein per kg body weight per day, distributed across 3-4 meals. The RDA of 0.8 g/kg is a minimum to prevent deficiency, not optimal for health and longevity.

Understanding Your Protein Needs

Quick Answer: Adults 40+ should aim for 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day. The RDA of 0.8 g/kg is a minimum to prevent deficiency—not optimal for maintaining muscle, strength, or longevity.

The current RDA (0.8 g/kg/day) was established to prevent protein deficiency in sedentary adults—it was never designed to optimize health, preserve muscle mass, or support active aging.

Evidence consistently shows higher intakes are beneficial:

PROT-AGE Study Group Position Paper
Bauer et al., 2013 • JAMDA • 14(8):542-559 • doi:10.1016/j.jamda.2013.05.021
International expert consensus: Older adults need 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day minimum, with 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day for those with acute/chronic illness or engaged in exercise.
View on PubMed →

Han, Woo & Kim, 2024 • Nutrients • 16(24):4350 • doi:10.3390/nu16244350
Adults consuming <0.8 g/kg/day had significantly higher sarcopenia risk compared to those eating ≥1.2 g/kg/day.

Following 2,000+ older adults over 3 years: Those in the lowest protein intake group lost 40% more muscle.

Quick Answer: No. While mechanistic theories (mTOR, IGF-1) suggest a possible link, large-scale human studies consistently find no association between protein intake and cancer mortality.
The Levine et al. Study—Why It's Misleading:

The oft-cited NHANES III study claiming 4× cancer risk in high-protein consumers aged 50-65 has significant problems:

  • The same study found high protein was associated with 60% lower cancer mortality after age 65
  • The high-protein group aged 50-65 likely had fewer than 35 cancer deaths—highly susceptible to chance
  • Couldn't adjust for cancer screening, family history, or other key variables
  • Multiple larger meta-analyses find no association
Protein and Cancer Mortality Meta-Analysis
Naghshi et al., 2020 • BMJ • 370:m2412 • doi:10.1136/bmj.m2412
Analyzed 12 prospective cohort studies (292,629 participants): Each 3% increment in energy from protein showed no association with cancer mortality.
View on PubMed →
German Nutrition Society Umbrella Review
Kühn et al., 2024 • European Journal of Nutrition • 63:1471-1486 • doi:10.1007/s00394-024-03380-4
Comprehensive review: Higher protein intake was not associated with risks of breast, prostate, colorectal, ovarian, or pancreatic cancer.
View on PubMed →
Why the mTOR Mechanism Doesn't Translate:

Protein activates mTORC1, but this is normal, tightly regulated physiology—not the chronic hyperactivation seen in cancer. Notably, resistance exercise activates mTORC1 even more than protein, yet is consistently associated with reduced cancer risk.

"For adults 40+, the evidence supports protein intake of 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day. The theoretical cancer concerns are not supported by human data, while the benefits for muscle preservation, metabolic health, and longevity are well-established. Getting too little protein poses a far greater health risk than getting too much." — Peter Attia, MD (recommends 1.6-2.0 g/kg/day)

Kidney Function Meta-Analysis
Devries et al., 2018 • J Nutr • 148(11):1760-1775 • doi:10.1093/jn/nxy197
28 RCTs (1,358 participants): Higher protein intakes (≥1.5 g/kg/day) do not adversely affect kidney function in adults without pre-existing kidney disease.

Important Exception: If you have diagnosed kidney disease or significantly reduced kidney function, discuss protein targets with your healthcare provider, as recommendations differ.
Quick Answer: It depends on your age. Due to "anabolic resistance," older adults need more protein per meal to trigger the same muscle-building response as younger people.

Moore et al. (2015, doi:10.1093/gerona/glu103) found that older adults need approximately 0.40 g/kg body weight per meal (vs 0.24 g/kg for young adults) to maximally stimulate muscle protein synthesis—about 68% more.

40 ~0.30 g/kg body weight ~21g
50 ~0.35 g/kg body weight ~25g
60 ~0.38 g/kg body weight ~27g
70+ ~0.40 g/kg body weight ~28-30g

Note: Moore et al. also reported values per kg lean body mass (0.60 g/kg LBM for older adults). The body weight values above are more practical for most people.

Protein Distribution Study
Mamerow et al., 2014 • J Nutr • 144(6):876-880 • doi:10.3945/jn.113.185280
Distributing protein evenly across meals (30g each) resulted in 25% higher 24-hour muscle protein synthesis compared to the same total protein in a skewed pattern (e.g., 10g breakfast, 20g lunch, 60g dinner).

Practical tip: Most people eat too little protein at breakfast. Switching from toast/cereal (5-10g) to eggs or Greek yogurt (20-30g) is often the highest-impact change.

Optimizing for Your Goals

Quick Answer: Increase protein when in a caloric deficit or doing resistance training. The harder you train or diet, the more protein you need to preserve muscle.
Your Situation Daily Target
No resistance training, maintaining weight 1.2 g/kg body weight
Resistance training, maintaining weight 1.6 g/kg body weight
Resistance training + moderate deficit (10-25%) 2.0 g/kg body weight
Resistance training + aggressive deficit (25-40%) 2.4 g/kg body weight
Body Weight vs. Lean Body Mass:

Research studies typically use total body weight, but leading specialists (Peter Attia, Layne Norton) recommend calculating protein based on lean body mass (LBM) instead. Why? Fat tissue has minimal protein requirements—lean mass explains 70-80% of metabolic rate. For people with higher body fat (>25% men, >35% women), using total body weight can give unnecessarily high targets. If you know your LBM, use these same numbers per kg LBM rather than body weight.

High Protein During Caloric Deficit
Longland et al., 2016 • Am J Clin Nutr • 103(3):738-746 • doi:10.3945/ajcn.115.119339
During a 40% caloric deficit (young trained men), 2.4 g/kg body weight/day produced dramatically better results: participants gained 1.2 kg lean mass while losing 4.8 kg fat, versus only +0.1 kg lean and −3.5 kg fat at 1.2 g/kg/day.
View on PubMed →

Protein + Strength Training Meta-Analysis
Tagawa et al., 2022 • Sports Medicine - Open • 8:110 • doi:10.1186/s40798-022-00508-w
Protein supplementation increased strength by 2.01% when combined with resistance training, but only 0.13% without training (not statistically significant).

Key numbers:

  • Each 0.1 g/kg/day increase adds 0.72% strength gain, up to 1.5 g/kg/day
  • Protein + training adds ~2.5 kg to your one-rep max vs. training alone
  • Protein + training adds ~0.3 kg lean mass vs. training alone

Plant vs. Animal Protein Bioavailability
Gorissen et al., 2018 • Amino Acids • 50(12):1685-1695 • doi:10.1007/s00726-018-2640-5
Plant proteins have lower leucine content and digestibility. To achieve the same muscle-building response, consume 20-30% more total protein from plant sources, or aim for 35-40g per meal instead of 30g.
View on PubMed →

Strategies for plant-based eaters:

  • Emphasize high-leucine plant foods: soy, lentils, peanuts
  • Combine complementary proteins at meals (legumes + grains)
  • Aim for 35-40g protein per meal instead of 30g
  • Consider a leucine supplement to match animal protein response
Quick Answer: Not necessarily—supplements are a convenience, not a requirement. Many people successfully meet 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day through food alone.

However, supplements can help if you:

  • Struggle with appetite (common with aging)
  • Find it difficult to eat 30+ grams at breakfast
  • Need portable options for travel or work
  • Follow dietary restrictions that limit protein sources
  • Practice intermittent fasting or time-restricted eating
Caution: Fasting and Protein

Peter Attia notes that fasting and time-restricted eating can be "problematic due to the heightened risk of protein deficiency and subsequent loss of muscle mass." If you practice intermittent fasting, ensure you still hit your total daily protein target. For muscle preservation during extended fasts, some specialists recommend prioritizing protein in your eating window—potentially using supplements to reach 1.6+ g/kg in fewer meals.

If you do supplement: Whey protein is well-studied and effective. Plant-based options like pea or soy protein work too but may benefit from added leucine.

Key Recommendations

Daily target: 1.2-1.6 g/kg body weight (higher if training or in deficit)

Per-meal target: 25-40g depending on age (older adults need more)

Distribution: Spread protein evenly across 3-4 meals for 25% better muscle synthesis
