How much protein do I really need for longevity?

4 min read · Based on 3 studies · 1 expert cited
Most adults benefit from 1.2–1.6 g per kg of body weight daily—roughly double the outdated RDA of 0.8 g/kg...
Quick Answer
Most adults benefit from 1.2–1.6 g per kg of body weight daily—roughly double the outdated RDA of 0.8 g/kg. The optimal range depends on your age, activity level, and health goals.

The recommended dietary allowance for protein (0.8 g/kg/day) was established to prevent deficiency, not to optimize health span. A growing body of evidence suggests that higher intakes—particularly after age 40—support muscle preservation, bone density, metabolic health, and even cognitive function.

One of the leading researchers in this field puts it plainly:

SP
Dr. Stuart Phillips McMaster University, Kinesiology
Current evidence indicates intakes in the range of at least 1.2 to 1.6 g/kg/day of high-quality protein is a more ideal target for achieving optimal health outcomes in adults."
— Phillips et al., Applied Physiology, Nutrition, and Metabolism, 2016

That recommendation is backed by a comprehensive position paper from an international expert group:

Position Paper published in JAMDA
PROT-AGE Study Group: Evidence-Based Recommendations for Optimal Dietary Protein Intake
Bauer et al., 2013 · International consensus · DOI: 10.1016/j.jamda.2013.05.021
Key finding: Older adults need 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day minimum, with 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day for those with illness or engaged in exercise.
View Study

The consequences of inadequate protein become clear in long-term studies. The Health ABC Study followed over 2,000 older adults:

Cohort Study published in Am J Clin Nutr
Health ABC Study: Dietary Protein and Muscle Mass Loss
Houston et al., 2008 · n=2,066 · DOI: 10.1093/ajcn/87.1.150
Key finding: Over 3 years, those in the lowest protein intake group lost 40% more muscle mass compared to those with higher intakes.
View Study

More recently, a meta-analysis confirmed the link between low protein and sarcopenia risk:

Meta-Analysis published in Nutrients
Protein Intake and Sarcopenia Risk: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
Han, Woo & Kim, 2024 · Multiple studies pooled · DOI: 10.3390/nu16244350
Key finding: Adults consuming <0.8 g/kg/day had significantly higher sarcopenia risk compared to those eating ≥1.2 g/kg/day.
View Study

In practical terms, a 70 kg (154 lb) person should aim for roughly 84–112 g of protein per day, spread across 3–4 meals. For older adults or those doing resistance training, the higher end—or even up to 2.0 g/kg—may offer additional benefits.

Practical Tip
Start by tracking your current protein for 3 days. Most people discover they're getting 40–60% of the optimal range. Simple swaps—Greek yogurt at breakfast, adding legumes to lunch—can close the gap without overhauling your diet.

Will high protein intake increase my cancer risk?

3 min read · Based on 1 study · 1 expert cited
No. While mechanistic theories suggest a possible link, large-scale human studies consistently find no association...
Quick Answer
No. While mechanistic theories (mTOR, IGF-1) suggest a possible link, large-scale human studies consistently find no association between protein intake and cancer mortality.

You may have seen headlines claiming high protein increases cancer risk. These often reference a single study (Levine et al., 2014) which has significant methodological problems:

The Levine et al. Study—Why It's Misleading
  • The same study found high protein was associated with 60% lower cancer mortality after age 65
  • The high-protein group aged 50-65 likely had fewer than 35 cancer deaths—highly susceptible to chance
  • Multiple larger meta-analyses find no association

The most comprehensive analysis to date examined 12 prospective cohort studies:

Meta-Analysis published in BMJ
Dietary Protein Intake and Risk of All-Cause, Cardiovascular, and Cancer Mortality
Naghshi et al., 2020 · n=292,629 · DOI: 10.1136/bmj.m2412
Key finding: Each 3% increment in energy from protein showed no association with cancer mortality. Higher total protein was actually linked to lower all-cause mortality.
View Study

What about the mTOR pathway? While protein activates mTORC1, this is normal, tightly regulated physiology. Notably, resistance exercise activates mTORC1 even more than protein, yet exercise is consistently associated with reduced cancer risk.

PA
Dr. Peter Attia Physician, Author of Outlive
For adults 40+, the evidence supports protein intake of 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day. The theoretical cancer concerns are not supported by human data, while the benefits for muscle preservation are well-established. I personally aim for 1.6–2.0 g/kg/day."

Is high protein bad for my kidneys?

2 min read · Based on 1 study
Not if your kidneys are healthy. Higher protein does not adversely affect kidney function in adults without pre-existing disease...
Quick Answer
Not if your kidneys are healthy. The concern originated from observations that protein restriction helps people with existing kidney disease—but this doesn't mean protein damages healthy kidneys.

This myth persists because people confuse two different things: managing existing kidney disease vs. causing kidney damage. A comprehensive meta-analysis addressed this directly:

Meta-Analysis published in J Nutr
Changes in Kidney Function Do Not Differ Between Healthy Adults Consuming Higher vs Lower Protein Diets
Devries et al., 2018 · 28 RCTs, n=1,358 · DOI: 10.1093/jn/nxy197
Key finding: Higher protein intakes (≥1.5 g/kg/day) do not adversely affect kidney function in adults without pre-existing kidney disease.
View Study
Important Exception

If you have diagnosed kidney disease or significantly reduced kidney function (eGFR <60), discuss protein targets with your healthcare provider, as recommendations differ.

How much protein should I eat per meal?

3 min read · Based on 2 studies · 1 expert cited
Aim for 25–40 g per meal, depending on your age. Older adults need more protein per meal to trigger the same muscle-building response...
Quick Answer
Aim for 25–40 g per meal, depending on your age. Due to "anabolic resistance," older adults need more protein per meal to trigger the same muscle-building response as younger people.

Your body can only use so much protein at once for muscle building. But that threshold increases with age due to a phenomenon called anabolic resistance:

Meta-Analysis published in Journal of Gerontology Series A
Protein Ingestion to Stimulate Myofibrillar Protein Synthesis Requires Greater Protein Intakes in Healthy Older Adults
Moore et al., 2015 · Dose-response analysis · DOI: 10.1093/gerona/glu103
Key finding: Older adults need approximately 0.40 g/kg body weight per meal (vs 0.24 g/kg for young adults) to maximally stimulate muscle protein synthesis—about 68% more.
View Study

Timing matters too. Research shows distributing protein evenly throughout the day, rather than concentrating it at dinner, produces better results:

RCT published in Journal of Nutrition
Dietary Protein Distribution Positively Influences Muscle Protein Synthesis
Mamerow et al., 2014 · Controlled crossover · DOI: 10.3945/jn.113.185280
Key finding: Distributing protein evenly across meals (30g each) resulted in 25% higher 24-hour muscle protein synthesis compared to the same total protein in a skewed pattern.
View Study

The most impactful change for most people is simply increasing protein at breakfast and lunch, where intake tends to be lowest:

DL
Dr. Don Layman University of Illinois, Nutrition Science
Most people eat 10 grams of protein at breakfast, 15 at lunch, and 60 at dinner. Flipping that pattern—front-loading protein earlier in the day—is one of the simplest high-impact changes you can make."
Practical Tip
Switch your breakfast from toast or cereal (5-10g protein) to eggs or Greek yogurt (20-30g). This single change can dramatically improve your protein distribution.

How do I adjust protein for weight loss or muscle building?

2 min read · Based on 1 study
Increase protein when dieting or training. The harder you train or diet, the more protein you need to preserve muscle...
Quick Answer
Increase protein when in a caloric deficit or doing resistance training. The harder you train or diet, the more protein you need to preserve muscle.
Your SituationDaily Target
No resistance training, maintaining weight1.2 g/kg
Resistance training, maintaining weight1.6 g/kg
Training + moderate deficit (10-25%)2.0 g/kg
Training + aggressive deficit (25-40%)2.4 g/kg

The importance of higher protein during weight loss was demonstrated dramatically in this study:

RCT published in Am J Clin Nutr
Higher vs. Lower Dietary Protein During an Energy Deficit Combined with Exercise
Longland et al., 2016 · 4-week intervention · DOI: 10.3945/ajcn.115.119339
Key finding: During a 40% caloric deficit, the high-protein group (2.4 g/kg/day) gained 1.2 kg lean mass while losing 4.8 kg fat. The lower protein group (1.2 g/kg) only gained 0.1 kg lean mass and lost 3.5 kg fat.
View Study
Key Insight
When dieting, protein becomes even more important because your body is more likely to break down muscle for energy. Higher protein protects your hard-earned muscle.

Does protein intake enhance the effects of strength training?

2 min read · Based on 1 study
Yes—protein and resistance training are synergistic. Protein alone doesn't build strength, but it significantly amplifies training effects...
Quick Answer
Yes—protein and resistance training are synergistic. Protein alone doesn't build strength, but it significantly amplifies the effects of training.
Meta-Analysis published in Sports Medicine - Open
Protein Supplementation and Strength Training: A Meta-Analysis of 78 RCTs
Tagawa et al., 2022 · n=4,863 · DOI: 10.1186/s40798-022-00508-w
Key finding: Protein supplementation increased strength by 2.01% when combined with resistance training, but only 0.13% without training (not statistically significant).
View Study
Key Numbers
Each 0.1 g/kg/day increase adds 0.72% strength gain (up to 1.5 g/kg/day). Protein + training adds ~2.5 kg to your one-rep max and ~0.3 kg lean mass vs. training alone.

Can I meet protein targets with plant protein alone?

2 min read · Based on 1 study
Yes, but it requires more planning. Plant proteins have lower leucine content, so you may need 20-30% more total protein...
Quick Answer
Yes, but it requires more planning. Plant proteins have lower leucine content and digestibility, so you may need 20-30% more total protein.
Review published in Amino Acids
Protein Content and Amino Acid Composition of Commercially Available Plant-Based Protein Isolates
Gorissen et al., 2018 · Comparative analysis · DOI: 10.1007/s00726-018-2640-5
Key finding: Plant proteins have lower leucine content and digestibility. To achieve the same muscle-building response, consume 20-30% more total protein from plant sources, or aim for 35-40g per meal instead of 30g.
View Study
Strategies for Plant-Based Eaters
1. Emphasize high-leucine plant foods: soy, lentils, peanuts
2. Combine complementary proteins at meals (legumes + grains)
3. Aim for 35-40g protein per meal instead of 30g
4. Consider a leucine supplement to match animal protein response

Do I need protein supplements to hit these targets?

2 min read · 1 expert cited
Not necessarily—supplements are a convenience, not a requirement. Many people meet targets through food alone...
Quick Answer
Not necessarily—supplements are a convenience, not a requirement. Many people successfully meet 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day through food alone.

However, supplements can help if you:

  • Struggle with appetite (common with aging)
  • Find it difficult to eat 30+ grams at breakfast
  • Need portable options for travel or work
  • Follow dietary restrictions that limit protein sources
  • Practice intermittent fasting or time-restricted eating
Caution: Fasting and Protein

Peter Attia notes that fasting and time-restricted eating can be "problematic due to the heightened risk of protein deficiency and subsequent loss of muscle mass." If you practice intermittent fasting, ensure you still hit your total daily protein target.

If You Do Supplement
Whey protein is well-studied and effective. Plant-based options like pea or soy protein work too but may benefit from added leucine.

All studies are peer-reviewed and published in high-impact medical journals.
