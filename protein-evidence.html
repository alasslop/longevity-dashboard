Understanding Your Protein Needs

Quick Answer

Adults 40+ should aim for 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day. The RDA of 0.8 g/kg is a minimum to prevent deficiency—not optimal for maintaining muscle, strength, or longevity.

The current RDA (0.8 g/kg/day) was established to prevent protein deficiency in sedentary adults—it was never designed to optimize health, preserve muscle mass, or support active aging.

PROT-AGE Study Group Position Paper
Bauer et al., 2013 • JAMDA • doi:10.1016/j.jamda.2013.05.021
Key finding: International expert consensus: Older adults need 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day minimum, with 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day for those with illness or engaged in exercise.
View on PubMed
Protein and Sarcopenia Meta-Analysis
Han, Woo & Kim, 2024 • Nutrients • doi:10.3390/nu16244350
Key finding: Adults consuming <0.8 g/kg/day had significantly higher sarcopenia risk compared to those eating ≥1.2 g/kg/day.
View on PubMed
Health ABC Study
Houston et al., 2008 • Am J Clin Nutr • doi:10.1093/ajcn/87.1.150
Key finding: Following 2,000+ older adults over 3 years: Those in the lowest protein intake group lost 40% more muscle.
View on PubMed

The Levine et al. Study—Why It's Misleading

The oft-cited NHANES III study claiming 4× cancer risk in high-protein consumers aged 50-65 has significant problems:

  • The same study found high protein was associated with 60% lower cancer mortality after age 65
  • The high-protein group aged 50-65 likely had fewer than 35 cancer deaths—highly susceptible to chance
  • Multiple larger meta-analyses find no association
Protein and Cancer Mortality Meta-Analysis
Naghshi et al., 2020 • BMJ • doi:10.1136/bmj.m2412
Key finding: Analyzed 12 prospective cohort studies (292,629 participants): Each 3% increment in energy from protein showed no association with cancer mortality.
View on PubMed
Why the mTOR Mechanism Doesn't Translate

Protein activates mTORC1, but this is normal, tightly regulated physiology. Notably, resistance exercise activates mTORC1 even more than protein, yet is consistently associated with reduced cancer risk.

"For adults 40+, the evidence supports protein intake of 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day. The theoretical cancer concerns are not supported by human data, while the benefits for muscle preservation are well-established." — Peter Attia, MD (recommends 1.6-2.0 g/kg/day)

Important Exception

If you have diagnosed kidney disease or significantly reduced kidney function, discuss protein targets with your healthcare provider, as recommendations differ.

Practical Tip

Optimizing for Your Goals

Quick Answer

Increase protein when in a caloric deficit or doing resistance training. The harder you train or diet, the more protein you need to preserve muscle.

Your SituationDaily Target
No resistance training, maintaining weight1.2 g/kg
Resistance training, maintaining weight1.6 g/kg
Training + moderate deficit (10-25%)2.0 g/kg
Training + aggressive deficit (25-40%)2.4 g/kg
High Protein During Caloric Deficit
Longland et al., 2016 • Am J Clin Nutr • doi:10.3945/ajcn.115.119339
Key finding: During a 40% caloric deficit, 2.4 g/kg/day produced dramatically better results: gained 1.2 kg lean mass while losing 4.8 kg fat, versus only +0.1 kg lean and −3.5 kg fat at 1.2 g/kg/day.
View on PubMed
Quick Answer

Yes—protein and resistance training are synergistic. Protein alone doesn't build strength, but it significantly amplifies the effects of training.

Protein + Strength Training Meta-Analysis
Tagawa et al., 2022 • Sports Medicine - Open • doi:10.1186/s40798-022-00508-w
Key finding: Protein supplementation increased strength by 2.01% when combined with resistance training, but only 0.13% without training (not statistically significant).
View on PubMed
Key Numbers
  • Each 0.1 g/kg/day increase adds 0.72% strength gain, up to 1.5 g/kg/day
  • Protein + training adds ~2.5 kg to your one-rep max vs. training alone
  • Protein + training adds ~0.3 kg lean mass vs. training alone

Quick Answer

Yes, but it requires more planning. Plant proteins have lower leucine content and digestibility, so you may need 20-30% more total protein.

Plant vs. Animal Protein Bioavailability
Gorissen et al., 2018 • Amino Acids • doi:10.1007/s00726-018-2640-5
Key finding: Plant proteins have lower leucine content and digestibility. To achieve the same muscle-building response, consume 20-30% more total protein from plant sources, or aim for 35-40g per meal instead of 30g.
View on PubMed
Strategies for Plant-Based Eaters
  • Emphasize high-leucine plant foods: soy, lentils, peanuts
  • Combine complementary proteins at meals (legumes + grains)
  • Aim for 35-40g protein per meal instead of 30g
  • Consider a leucine supplement to match animal protein response
Quick Answer

Not necessarily—supplements are a convenience, not a requirement. Many people successfully meet 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day through food alone.

However, supplements can help if you:

  • Struggle with appetite (common with aging)
  • Find it difficult to eat 30+ grams at breakfast
  • Need portable options for travel or work
  • Follow dietary restrictions that limit protein sources
  • Practice intermittent fasting or time-restricted eating
Caution: Fasting and Protein

Peter Attia notes that fasting and time-restricted eating can be "problematic due to the heightened risk of protein deficiency and subsequent loss of muscle mass." If you practice intermittent fasting, ensure you still hit your total daily protein target.

If You Do Supplement

Whey protein is well-studied and effective. Plant-based options like pea or soy protein work too but may benefit from added leucine.

All studies are peer-reviewed and published in high-impact medical journals.
