I scored below 25% on protein — what does that mean for my health?

3 min read
Insufficient protein intake accelerates muscle loss, weakens bones, and undermines weight management — but increasing intake is one of the most straightforward nutrition changes you can make...
Quick Answer
A low protein score suggests your intake may be below the optimal range for longevity. Adults consuming insufficient protein lose 40% more muscle mass over three years compared to those with adequate intake. Low protein also impairs recovery from exercise, reduces satiety, and accelerates age-related decline. The standard RDA of 0.8 g/kg/day prevents deficiency but does not optimize health — most longevity researchers now recommend 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day.

Muscle Preservation Lowest protein intake group lost 40% more muscle mass over 3 years than highest group. Houston 2008, n=2,066 [details →]
Sarcopenia Risk Adults consuming <0.8 g/kg/day have significantly higher sarcopenia risk vs. ≥1.2 g/kg/day. Han 2024, MA [details →]

Muscle During Dieting In a caloric deficit, high protein (2.4 g/kg) gained 1.2 kg lean mass while losing 4.8 kg fat. Longland 2016, RCT [details →]

The consequences of low protein compound with age. After 40, the body becomes progressively less efficient at using dietary protein for muscle building — a phenomenon called anabolic resistance. This means you need more protein as you age, not less, to maintain the same muscle mass and metabolic health.

How much protein do I really need for longevity?

4 min read
Most adults benefit from 1.2–1.6 g per kg of body weight daily—roughly double the outdated RDA of 0.8 g/kg...
Quick Answer
Most adults benefit from 1.2–1.6 g per kg of body weight daily—roughly double the outdated RDA of 0.8 g/kg. The optimal range depends on your age, activity level, and health goals.

The recommended dietary allowance for protein (0.8 g/kg/day) was established to prevent deficiency, not to optimize health span. A growing body of evidence suggests that higher intakes—particularly after age 40—support muscle preservation, bone density, metabolic health, and even cognitive function [1].

Will high protein intake increase my cancer risk?

3 min read
No. While mechanistic theories suggest a possible link, large-scale human studies consistently find no association...
Quick Answer
No. While mechanistic theories (mTOR, IGF-1) suggest a possible link, large-scale human studies consistently find no association between protein intake and cancer mortality.

You may have seen headlines claiming high protein increases cancer risk. These often reference a single study (Levine et al., 2014) which has significant methodological problems [1]:

The most comprehensive analysis to date examined 12 prospective cohort studies [1]:

What about the mTOR pathway? While protein activates mTORC1, this is normal, tightly regulated physiology. Notably, resistance exercise activates mTORC1 even more than protein, yet exercise is consistently associated with reduced cancer risk [1].

Key finding: Plant protein associated with 5% lower all-cause mortality per 3% energy substitution; no association between total protein and cancer mortality

Is high protein bad for my kidneys?

2 min read
Not if your kidneys are healthy. Higher protein does not adversely affect kidney function in adults without pre-existing disease...
Quick Answer
Not if your kidneys are healthy. The concern originated from observations that protein restriction helps people with existing kidney disease—but this doesn't mean protein damages healthy kidneys.

This myth persists because people confuse two different things: managing existing kidney disease vs. causing kidney damage. A comprehensive meta-analysis addressed this directly [1]:

Aim for 25–40 g per meal, depending on your age. Older adults need more protein per meal to trigger the same muscle-building response...
Quick Answer
Aim for 25–40 g per meal, depending on your age. Due to "anabolic resistance," older adults need more protein per meal to trigger the same muscle-building response as younger people.

Your body can only use so much protein at once for muscle building. But that threshold increases with age due to a phenomenon called anabolic resistance [1]:

Quick Answer
Increase protein when in a caloric deficit or doing resistance training. The harder you train or diet, the more protein you need to preserve muscle.
