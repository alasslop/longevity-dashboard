Scientific evidence

A push-up test combines upper body strength, core stability, and muscular endurance in a single movement. Your score is a window into the condition of your whole muscular system â€” not just your chest and arms. The number you can complete reflects relative strength, cardiovascular conditioning, and body composition all at once, which is why it predicts health outcomes far beyond what a single muscle group could explain.

Yang et al. followed 1,104 active men for 10 years and found that those completing 40 or more push-ups had 96% fewer cardiovascular events than those managing fewer than 10 [S85]. The effect was dramatic: an incidence rate ratio of 0.04 (95% CI 0.01â€“0.36) â€” meaning the high-capacity group had almost no cardiovascular events over a decade. Push-ups predicted heart disease better than submaximal treadmill testing in the same cohort.

What does my chair stand score tell me about my risk of losing independence?

2 min read

Scientific evidence

Your chair stand score measures lower body muscular endurance â€” how many times your legs can produce enough force to lift you out of a chair before they fatigue. This is different from maximal strength. It reflects the repeated, sustained effort your body needs for everyday movements: getting up from a sofa, climbing a flight of stairs, stepping off a bus. After age 60, these capacities determine whether you remain independent or start needing help.

Rikli & Jones tested over 7,000 community-dwelling adults aged 60â€“94 and established criterion-referenced fitness standards â€” specific performance thresholds below which a person is at risk of losing the ability to carry out daily tasks without assistance [S83]. If you score Below Average, your functional endurance reserve is declining. Average and above means your legs can handle the repeated demands of daily life with increasing margin to spare.

How to take the test

The test uses a standard-height chair (approximately 43 cm / 17 inches seat height) without armrests. Start seated with feet flat on the floor, arms crossed at the chest. Stand up fully â€” hips and knees extended â€” then sit back down so your weight is fully on the seat. That is one complete rep. Count as many complete stand-sit cycles as you can in 30 seconds. Partial reps (not fully standing or not fully sitting) do not count.

Quick Answer
Women lose muscle mass and strength faster after menopause due to estrogen decline. But relative to their starting point, training response is similar. Both sexes benefit equally from resistance trainingâ€”in percentage terms.

The gender difference in strength decline is real and significant. Post-menopause, women can lose 1% of muscle mass per year, accelerating the decline that begins around age 40. The meta-analysis on grip strength showed women actually have stronger protective association with strength than men:

Why not use grip strength instead of push-ups, since it predicts longevity?

2 min read
Grip strength IS an excellent mortality predictor, but push-ups test integrated function. Both are valuable...
Quick Answer
Grip strength is an excellent predictor of mortality. Push-ups test integrated upper body function. Use both: grip strength is a marker, push-ups are a functional exercise. Neither replaces full-body training.

Grip strength is genuinely one of the best single-measure predictors of mortality in the literature. The Celis-Morales study on 500,000+ people showed it outperforms many other risk factors. But push-ups capture something different: functional integration.

Push-ups require coordinated activation of chest, shoulders, triceps, and core. You're pushing against gravity, controlling your body weight, and maintaining spinal alignment. This functional demand is why push-ups predict cardiovascular healthâ€”they reveal systemic capacity, not just isolated muscle strength.

Grip strength, by contrast, tests the forearm and hand muscles in isolation. It's a powerful markerâ€”correlating with overall muscle mass and physical capacity. But a strong grip doesn't require core stability, shoulder mobility, or pushing power. The benefit of grip testing is that it requires no equipment and takes seconds, making it practical for widespread screening.
